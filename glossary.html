Glossary

Causal Ambiguity

When the connection between a firm’s resources and its success is unclear, making it difficult for competitors to imitate.

Example: A bakery is successful, but competitors don’t know if it’s because of the ingredients, the recipe, or customer service.

Competitive Advantage

A firm stands out from its competitors due to cost, focus, or differentiation.

Example: A local pizza shop makes faster deliveries than its competitors, attracting more customers.

Core Competencies

Defining characteristics that allow a firm to stand out against competitors and sustain a competitive edge.

Example:A shoe company is known for making the most comfortable sneakers.

Dynamic Capabilities

The ability of a firm to improve, update, or create resources in response to changing environments.

Example: Netflix transitioned from DVD rentals to streaming due to changing technology and consumer habits.

Intangible Resources

Non-physical assets such as brand reputation, intellectual property, and corporate culture that provide competitive advantage.

Example: Google’s proprietary search algorithms give it a competitive edge.

Resource-Based Theory

A framework where a firm leverages its internal resources and capabilities as a competitive advantage.

Example: Coca-Cola’s secret soda recipe and global brand recognition.

Resource Heterogeneity

The concept that each firm possesses unique resources that are not perfectly transferable.

Example: Starbucks has both a strong global brand and unique local offerings.

Resource Immobility

Some resources cannot be easily transferred or replicated by competitors.

Example: Ferrari’s brand prestige and engineering expertise.

Resources

Assets that a firm owns or controls, which can be tangible (physical) or intangible (non-physical).

Example: A farmer’s land and farming expertise.

Tangible Resources

Example: Toyota’s factories and manufacturing equipment.

VRIO Framework

A framework to assess whether a firm’s resources lead to sustainable competitive advantage based on Value, Rarity, Imitability, and Organization.

  • Value: Does it create value?
  • Rarity: Is it unique?
  • Imitability: Is it difficult to copy?
  • Organization: Is the firm organized to exploit it?

Example: Apple’s iOS—valuable, rare, costly to imitate, and well-organized within Apple’s ecosystem.
